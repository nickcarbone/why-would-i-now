Florasongs — Critical Writing
Essay The Decemberists 2015

Essay  •  Florasongs EP  •  Outtake Criticism

Why Would
I Now?

The Decemberists

The Decemberists' quietly buried outtake says more with rhetorical questions than most songs manage with declarations — and its plainness is the whole point.

By  Nick

Published  2025

There's a particular kind of loyalty that doesn't announce itself. It doesn't build to a chorus, it doesn't reach for a key change, and it definitely doesn't tell you it loves you. It just turns to you — after years, after the hard stretches, after the times it loathed your name — and asks a quiet, slightly bewildering question: why would I start failing you now?

That's the entire architecture of "Why Would I Now?", the opening track from the Decemberists' 2015 EP Florasongs — a five-song collection of outtakes from the What a Terrible World, What a Beautiful World sessions. The fact that it didn't make the album is worth sitting with, not because it's weaker material, but because it may have been too emotionally undefended for a record already pushing against the band's own comfort zone. Those sessions were the band's first after a four-year hiatus Colin Meloy spent largely writing and parenting, and the album wears that domestic reckoning visibly. "Why Would I Now?" goes even further — strips away the historical distance, the archaic vocabulary, the conceptual scaffolding — and says something plainly. For Meloy, that's an unusual risk.

I  —  Loyalty as Negative Proof

The song's entire rhetorical structure is built on negative questions. Every verse runs the same move: Have I ever done X? No. Then why would I now? Meloy never says "I will always be there for you." He doesn't promise anything about the future. Instead, he presents a record of conduct:

Have I ever lost your way?
Have I ever left you waylaid?
Have I ever come unstayed?
Why would I now?

This framing is more persuasive — and more honest — than a direct declaration would be. Declarations of love live in the present tense and make claims about the future. They're aspirational. A record of conduct is verifiable. It appeals to shared history rather than present feeling. The implicit argument: you don't need to trust my intentions, just look at what I've actually done.

It avoids sentimentality by design. "I will always be there for you" is a promise that can be broken. "Have I ever not been there?" is a question with an accumulated answer.

II  —  The "There Were Times" Stanzas

This is where the song earns its complexity. Meloy doesn't just stack up credentials as a loyal partner or friend. He admits real failure:

There were times I loathed your name
There were times I laid on the blame
There were times I brought on shame
But why would I now?

The "but" in that final line is doing quiet, significant work. It holds the admissions and the commitment in the same breath without dissolving the tension between them. He's not saying despite those failures he stayed. He's saying those failures are the relationship — the actual texture of it, not the exception — and staying is still the answer.

Most loyalty-themed songs either erase the difficult material or relegate it to a bridge that resolves narratively. Here, the bad stuff is structural. It's in every verse. That's a notably mature emotional register, and a notably honest one.

III  —  The Chorus: Limitation as a Form of Love

The chorus is where Meloy's literary instincts reassert themselves — and usefully so, because these two lines carry the most weight in the song:

For I will never be your familiar soul
And I will never see your imperial hold

"Familiar" operates on two frequencies simultaneously. In the everyday sense, it means fully known, fully understood. In the older esoteric sense — one Meloy, who has spent his career trafficking in folk mythology and antiquarian reference, would absolutely be aware of — a familiar is a spirit bound to a person. Either reading lands: the speaker acknowledging they'll never be entirely knowable to the other person, or that they'll never achieve the kind of merged, symbiotic intimacy that erases individual edges.

"Imperial hold" is harder. Empire, dominion, authority. The speaker seems to be acknowledging that the other person's interiority — whatever it is that draws them, holds them, makes the relationship feel necessary — exceeds their full comprehension. They can't see it whole. They can't map it.

Taken together, these lines are a concession of limitation placed inside a declaration of commitment. I will never fully know you. I will never understand what you are to me. And the "but" that follows: none of that changes what I do. Action over understanding. Presence over comprehension.

That's not a romantic's argument — it's a realist's. And it's more durable for it.

IV  —  Who Is Being Addressed?

The song refuses to close this question, which feels deliberate. The most obvious reading is a long-term romantic partner — the admissions of resentment and shame, the broken-through walls, the durability through difficulty, all map cleanly onto a marriage or long partnership with genuine weather in it.

But "familiar soul" and "imperial hold" carry a slight formality that doesn't quite settle into domestic intimacy, and an old friendship — one where the emotional vocabulary is less practiced, more bounded — fits nearly as well. There's also a more interesting reading beneath both: the song addressed to a child. I will never be your familiar soul takes on different weight when a parent is watching someone become a person they can't entirely know. There were times I loathed your name becomes something rawer, more honest about the unreported difficulty of early parenthood. Meloy became a father during the years before these sessions; WTAWABW is threaded with domesticity in ways his earlier work rarely was. A parent's commitment — I have never stood back and watched you fall, so why would I now? — carries its own particular gravity.

One more possibility lurks at the edges. WTAWABW opens with "The Singer Addresses His Audience," Meloy's drolly meta-commentary on the tension between artistic evolution and fan expectation. "Why Would I Now?" could be the other side of that conversation — not the apology for having changed, but the assurance that changing doesn't mean leaving. Have I ever not shown up for you?

All of these readings can coexist. The song is better for it.

V  —  The Plain Style and What It Signals

Meloy built his reputation on vocabulary that sends listeners to dictionaries — palanquins, minarets, charlatans, fontanelles. His songs typically approach emotion at an angle: through historical narrative, mythological character, deliberate anachronism. "Why Would I Now?" is written almost entirely in the vernacular. "Have I ever steered you wrong? / Have I ever strung you along?" — idiomatic, conversational, no armor.

For a songwriter whose default mode is elaborate historical costume, that plainness registers as exposure. The departure from his own aesthetic is itself a signal: when Meloy writes without the masks, something actual is being said. This is likely part of why the song became an outtake — not because it's weaker, but because it's less defended, and the album it was cut from was already pushing against the band's comfort zone. Released quietly on an EP of session leftovers, it found exactly the right container: small, unhurried, off to the side.

When Meloy writes without the masks, something actual is being said. The EP gave it the right container — small, unhurried, off to the side.

VI  —  The Emotional Argument

What the song is ultimately making is this case: commitment is not proven by declaration, it's proven by record. And that record doesn't have to be clean. The speaker has loathed, blamed, brought shame — and stayed anyway. The promise being offered is not the promise of someone for whom loyalty came easily. It's the promise of someone who knows exactly how hard it's been and is making it regardless.

The phrasing of the title matters. "Why would I now?" isn't triumphant. It has a note of weariness in it, even of quiet bewilderment — after everything we've been through, after everything I've admitted to, why would this be the moment I stop? The commitment has earned its cost rather than been offered freely from a position of ease.

That's what makes it one of the more affecting things Meloy has written: not the craft, which is considerable, but the fact that it doesn't hide behind the craft. It just asks its question, and waits.
